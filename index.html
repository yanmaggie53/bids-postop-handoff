The problem & objectives

When a patient leaves the operating room for the intensive care unit, a great deal of critical information has to travel with them: what happened during surgery, which medications are running, what the team should watch for next. That transfer of the patient and thier information is called a handoff, and it is one of the most crucial moments in a patient’s care.

Handoffs are often not standardized, inconsistent, and dependent on memory. Important details can be lost in the noise of moving a patient, connecting monitors, and simply just managing a fresh post-surgical case.

The goal of this QI study is to assess the feasilbility of the Epic-embedded postoperative handoff tool and to evaluate the perceived usability and effectiveness of the report tool in ensuing continuity of care during handoffs.

Data Collection Methodology

To understand the workflow, I have been observing the handoff process firsthand. I typically arrive in the OR towards the end of surgeries, so I get a chance to talk to the anesthesia team and encourage them to use the Epic-embedded handoff tool during the handoff process. Then, I'm taking notes on how the OR team works to prepare a patient for transfer to the ICU and keeping time stamps on the important actions being done.

I then follow the team as they transport the patient and observe and take notes on the setup of the patient in the ICU and how the OR team delivers their handoff report once both OR and ICU teams are ready.

Afterwards, if the ICU clinicans aren't too busy, I'll talk to them and try to get their feedback on the handoff tool as well as encouraging them to use it at any point in the handoff process.

Findings & Analysis

Data Analysis Methodology

After handwritting notes on the observations, the data was transcribed into a spreadsheet.

Every sentence is read through and openly coded for relevant statements on the postoperative handoff report use, barries and facilitators to the postoperative handoff workflow.

Sub-themes and overarching themes of clinician experiences were generated based on patterns, similarities, and overlap between codes. The following are the three main themes identified:

  • Perceived usefulness of the handoff report which encapsulate both the positive and negative feedback on the usefulness of the tool
  • Challenges with the workflow, tool itself, integrating the tool into the workflow, and clinician behavior
  • General suggestions for report improvement and implementation

Ongoing Work and Next Steps

The next step is to evaluate how effectively the new machine learning (ML) risk summary tool integrates into active clinical workflows. Moving forward, I will be helping to conduct the high-fidelity evaluation stage of the INTERACT (IntelligeNt inTERdisciplinAry care ContinuiTy) bundle usability study.

Phase 3: Usability Study

For this next phase, the goal is to evaluate a much more advanced version of the risk summary tool, based on feedback from previous phases. Next steps include:

  • Cognitive walkthroughs: Help conduct walkthroughs with clinicians, and observe and audio-record them as they perform handoff tasks like reviewing reports and documenting care plans.
  • Thematic analysis: Analyzing simulation notes and transcripts to code for cognitive/physical workload, workflow barriers, and unintended consequences to refine the tool for future clinical trials.
